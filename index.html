This network visualization shows the people Donald Trump mentions most often in his posts. Notably, most of these mentions are negative. The main exceptions are Trump himself and a few close family members, who are consistently talked about in a positive way. In fact, Trump often talks about himself more than anyone else—something that fits his pattern of self-aggrandizement. Those he speaks about negatively are often political rivals—primarily Democrats—but also include figures from within his own party and media allies. For instance, Trump has criticized Gregg Jarrett, a Fox News journalist and Republican politicians like Marco Rubio and Mitt Romney, especially when they opposed him or voiced criticism. Trump frequently refers to these individuals in groups. This can partly be explained by their involvement in related events, such as the Russia investigation or the impeachment proceedings. However, this pattern also supports Trump's broader narrative of positioning himself as an outsider battling a corrupt and entrenched elite.

Trump's most negative tweets weren't just louder, they traveled further. They were liked, shared, and picked up by the news. In doing so, these tweets helped shape what the public talked about and what politicians acted on.

But behind the likes and retweets lies a strategy, one designed to stir emotion, dominate attention, and set the agenda. So before you scroll or share, ask yourself: What's the purpose behind this post?
