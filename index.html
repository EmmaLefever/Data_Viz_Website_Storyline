For years, Donald Trump showed just how much influence a political figure can have, 140 characters at a time.

From bold claims to sharp insults, his Twitter feed became one of the main ways he shaped public opinion, made headlines, and even challenged the rules of online platforms.

To understand just how central Twitter became to Trump's political style, it helps to look at the numbers. Over the years, his tweeting didn't slow down, it accelerated, becoming a key part of how he communicated and shaped the news.

The graph highlights several critical turning points in Trump's Twitter usage. Following Obama's 2012 reelection, we observe the first major spike as Trump launched frequent attacks against Obama, Democrats, and what he termed "fake news."

By 2015, well before officially announcing his candidacy, Trump's tweet volume began rising significantly as he laid groundwork for his 2016 presidential run. During this period, he tested campaign messaging and increasingly focused on voter engagement. This timing coincided with the release of Celebrity Apprentice Season 7, contributing to increased tweets about media topics.

The most dramatic escalation occurred during the 2020 campaign, when Trump's Twitter activity reached unprecedented levels. A notable smaller spike appears in 2019, corresponding with his first impeachment proceedings. This pattern demonstrates how Twitter evolved from a supplementary communication channel to the centerpiece of Trump's political strategy and public persona.

But Trump's Twitter feed wasn't just about volume, it was also about what he chose to talk about. Certain themes kept popping up again and again, shaping the political conversation online. From attacks on the media to talk on immigration, let's take a closer look at the main topics that defined his tweeting and what they tell us about his strategy.

At first glance, it seems that Trump's tweets were mostly positive before he launched his successful presidential campaign in 2015. From 2015 onward, his negative tweets became more prominent.

Spikes in negative sentiment often seem to line up with key election periods, hinting at a deliberate shift in tone to rally supporters, attack opponents, and dominate the conversation.

While the timeline shows how Trump's tone shifted over time and adapted to key events, it's just as revealing to look at what he talked about and how he talked about it.

This visualization reveals a striking pattern in Trump's Twitter behavior: negativity dominated his most frequent topics. Democrats were his primary target with 8,636 tweets, consistently framed in negative sentiment. This criticism of political opponents outpaced even his calls for campaign votes (7,845 tweets), which carried positive sentiment.

Three of Trump's top five most tweeted topics display negative sentiment: attacks on Democrats, fake news (7,287 tweets), and political controversies (5,977 tweets). This demonstrates how criticism and conflict formed the foundation of his social media strategy. Economics (5,992 tweets) rounds out his top five topics.

The contrast between topics with positive sentiment (campaign votes, media, nationalism, support gratitude, luxury, and immigration) and his more numerous negative topics reveals how Trump strategically balanced criticism with promotion of his agenda.

Notably, the network visualization shows most mentions are negative.

The main exceptions are Trump himself and a few close family members, who are consistently talked about in a positive way. In fact, Trump often talks about himself more than anyone else, something that fits his pattern of self-aggrandizement. Those he speaks about negatively are often political rivals, primarily Democrats, but also include figures from within his own party and media allies. For instance, Trump has criticized Gregg Jarrett, a Fox News journalist and Republican politicians like Marco Rubio and Mitt Romney, especially when they opposed him or voiced criticism.

Trump frequently refers to individuals he speaks about negatively in groups. This can partly be explained by their involvement in related events, such as the Russia investigation or the impeachment proceedings. However, this pattern also supports Trump's broader narrative of positioning himself as an outsider battling a corrupt and entrenched elite.

These visualizations suggest that negative rhetoric consistently generated the highest engagement across both retweets and favorites. Political controversies dominated retweets with a staggering 94 million, followed closely by tweets about Democrats (91M) and fake news (65M). This pattern is mirrored in favorite counts, where tweets about Democrats led with 292 million favorites, followed by political controversies (255M) and economics (239M).

This data demonstrates that Trump's negative content outperformed his positive messaging in terms of audience engagement. While topics with positive sentiment like nationalism, immigration, and support gratitude also received interaction, they consistently lagged behind the controversial content. This disparity explains Trump's strategic emphasis on divisive rhetoric: it simply generated more attention and amplification through social media.

Trump's most negative tweets weren't just louder, they traveled further. They were liked, shared, and picked up by the news. In doing so, these tweets helped shape what the public talked about and what politicians acted on.

But behind the likes and retweets lies a strategy, one designed to stir emotion, dominate attention, and set the agenda. So before you scroll or share, ask yourself: What's the purpose behind this post?
